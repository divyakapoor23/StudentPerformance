Key Findings

Engagement is the strongest signal — but it isn't clean

Engagement

Clicks track outcome almost like a staircase

Average total clicks rise from 314 (Withdrawn) to 652 (Fail), 1,922 (Pass), and 2,667 (Distinction). Confirmed with Welch's t-test, not just raw averages (t ≈ 90.7, p ≈ 0).

Timing

Logging in before day one matters

Students who clicked anything before the official start date passed at 44.0% vs. 21.6% for everyone else, and withdrew at half the rate. Real, but moderate (Cramér's V = 0.288) — likely reflects motivation rather than early-login itself causing success.

Completion

Completion rate is closer to a definition than a predictor

Pass/Distinction students complete 87-88% of assessments; Withdrawn students complete only 29%. Consistent with withdrawal meaning "stopped partway through" — a caution explored further in the model comparison below.

Education

Prior qualification shifts the odds, doesn't decide them

Pass rate climbs from 29.7% (no formal quals) to 65.5% (postgraduate) — though the extreme groups are small (313-347 students) next to the 13-14k-strong middle categories.

Predictive Modeling

Two logistic regressions, one honest number

The first model uses full in-course behavior and looks great. The second drops anything that could only be known because the course already happened. The gap between them is the finding.

91.3%
Model 1 — includes completion_rate, total_clicks
  • Dominated by one coefficient: completion_rate
  • Mostly detects students who already stopped participating
  • Not a fair test of early prediction
64.6%
Model 2 — demographics + prior attempts + early engagement only
  • Balanced precision/recall (0.62–0.67) across both classes
  • engaged_before_start re-emerges as the top predictor
  • The realistic ceiling on early prediction from this data

Caveats
  • All relationships found are correlational — no causal claims can be made from this analysis alone.
  • Engagement metrics are themselves outcomes of student behavior, not independent causes — demonstrated directly by the 91% vs. 65% accuracy gap.
  • 65% accuracy is a more honest ceiling, but still modest — most of the variance in outcome isn't explained by demographics and early engagement alone.
  • Sample sizes vary a lot across subgroups (education level, region) — group sizes were checked before trusting any percentage.
